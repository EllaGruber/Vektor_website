Vektor: an evidence-based tool for early mathematics learning

Vektor is an adaptive training app developed at Karolinska Institutet. It combines visuospatial working memory training with number-line tasks to improve mathematical ability in children aged 6–9, based on randomised controlled trials with over 17,000 participants.

⚠️ This project currently targets Swedish schools, Grade 1 only.

The Research

Built on decades of cognitive neuroscience

Vektor emerged from Torkel Klingberg's lab at Karolinska Institutet, where research on working memory, attention, and mathematical learning has shaped evidence-based interventions for children.

The app adapts in real-time to each student's performance, ensuring appropriately challenging tasks that build confidence and fluency.

The Team

Researchers

This project is led by Ella Gruber (PhD student, KI) under the supervision of Prof. Torkel Klingberg, co-supervised by Ola Helenius, and in close collaboration with Prof. Bruce McCandliss.

Ella Gruber
PhD Student · Karolinska Institutet

Principal investigator of this project. Ella's doctoral research focuses on the integration of cognitive training and early mathematics education, combining adaptive learning technology with classroom-based interventions.

Prof. Torkel Klingberg
Founder of Vektor · Principal Supervisor · Karolinska Institutet

World-leading researcher in cognitive neuroscience and working memory. His group has led large-scale educational RCTs involving thousands of children, including the nationwide Vektor trials (Judd & Klingberg, 2021), and developed the AI-adaptive models underpinning this project.

klingberglab.se ↗
Ola Helenius
Co-Supervisor · University of Gothenburg

Leading expert in early mathematics education, currently leading the development of Sweden's national mathematics curriculum for young children. He runs several large-scale teaching intervention programmes funded by Vetenskapsrådet and MAW, ensuring the pedagogical integration and ecological validity of the intervention.

Prof. Bruce McCandliss
International Collaborator · Stanford University

Leading authority on early numerical cognition. His pioneering work established "groupitizing" as the strongest cognitive predictor of early mathematics achievement (Guillaume et al., 2023; Starkey & McCandliss, 2014). He acts as a close scientific advisor on the integration of groupitizing constructs into this project.

Training for measurable math gains
Nature Human Behaviour 2021

Training spatial cognition enhances mathematical learning in a randomized study of 17,000 children

Judd N., Klingberg T.

Across 17,648 children aged 6–8, visuospatial working memory and non-verbal reasoning training produced the greatest gains in math — more than spatial rotation. Strong evidence that cognitive training transfers to academic ability.

📄 doi: 10.1038/s41562-021-01118-4 ↗
J. Child Adolesc. Psychiatry 2005

Computerized training of working memory in children with ADHD — a randomized, controlled trial

Klingberg T., Fernell E., Olesen P.J., et al.

Landmark RCT demonstrating that adaptive, computerized working memory training improves WM capacity and reduces inattention symptoms in children with ADHD — the foundational study behind Vektor's adaptive engine.

📄 doi: 10.1097/00004583-200502000-00010 ↗
Psychological Research 2014

Effect of working memory training on working memory, arithmetic and following instructions

Bergman-Nutley S., Klingberg T.

In 176 children aged 7–15 with WM deficits, 5 weeks of training improved arithmetic and the ability to follow instructions — confirming that WM gains transfer to real academic tasks.

📄 doi: 10.1007/s00426-014-0614-0 ↗
Frontiers in Psychology 2015

Working memory training is associated with long-term attainments in math and reading

Söderqvist S., Bergman Nutley S.

Follow-up study showing that children who trained with the WM program showed significantly better math and reading outcomes more than a year later — evidence for durable, real-world academic benefits.

📄 doi: 10.3389/fpsyg.2015.01711 ↗
Cerebral Cortex 2012

Brain activity during a visuospatial working memory task predicts arithmetical performance 2 years later

Dumontheil I., Klingberg T.

Using neuroimaging, the study found that fronto-parietal brain activity during WM tasks in young children predicted their arithmetic ability two years later — revealing a neural basis for Vektor's approach.

📄 doi: 10.1093/cercor/bhr175 ↗
J. Clinical & Exp. Neuropsychology 2002

Training of working memory in children with ADHD

Klingberg T., Forssberg H., Westerberg H.

The original study establishing that working memory — long considered a fixed trait — can be improved through intensive adaptive training in children. The founding insight of the entire Vektor program.

📄 doi: 10.1076/jcen.24.6.781.8395 ↗

GDPR & Personal Data

Information for participating teachers and schools · Vektor Research Project 2026

🔒 What data is collected?

During the project, Vektor collects anonymised performance data (task scores, response times, and training progress) linked to each student's unique ID. No names, birthdates, or other directly identifying information are stored within the app.

📋 How to request or delete your data

Teachers and schools have the right to request access to all data collected for their students, or to request deletion of that data at any time. To do so, please contact the research team directly by email. We will respond within 30 days in accordance with GDPR Article 12.

✅ Consent — responsibility of schools and teachers

In accordance with GDPR and Swedish law, obtaining informed consent from parents or guardians for children's participation in this research project is the responsibility of the participating school and teacher. Before enrolling students in Vektor, teachers must ensure that appropriate consent has been collected and documented at the school level. The research team does not collect consent forms directly.

🏛️ Legal basis & data controller

Data processing is carried out on the basis of scientific research (GDPR Article 9(2)(j)) under the ethical oversight of Karolinska Institutet. The data controller is Karolinska Institutet, Department of Neuroscience.

Contact for data requests: Email us at [email protected] with subject line "Data Request — [School Name]".
